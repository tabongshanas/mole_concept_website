the mole concept

1.1 introduction

In ordinary day life items are counted using a named collection of them or counting units. For example, farmers count eggs in dozens (12), shop keepers sell sheets of papers in reams (500 papers). A gross represents 144 items while a score stands for 20 items. Elementary chemical particles (atoms, ions, molecules, elements etc) are very tiny. Chemist therefore, for convenient reasons count them by weighing a collection of them. The counting unit in chemistry is called the mole (6.02 x 1023 particles). The number of atoms in exactly 12 grams of the carbon-12 isotope is chosen as the standard.

The mole of a substance is the amount of substance that contains the same number of Elementary particles (atoms, ions, molecules) as there are atoms in exactly 12 grams of the carbon-12 isotope

Using the definition of the mole above, this means that:

  • 1 mole of Hydrogen atoms, H, contains 6.02 x 1023 hydrogen atoms, H.
  • 1 mole of oxygen molecules, O2, contains 6.02 x 1023 hydrogen molecules, O2.
  • 1 mole of oxygen atoms, O, contains 6.02 x 1023 hydrogen molecules, O.
  • 1 mole of electrons, e- contains 6.02 x 1023 electrons.

It is important to always refer to what the particle is. For example, "1 mole of oxygen" could refer to oxygen atoms (O) or to oxygen molecules (O2). It is always safest to quote the formula to which the amount of substance refer.

a) Relative Atomic Mass (Ar)

Masses of atoms are very small (10-24 to 10-22g). One atom of hydrogen has a mass of 1.66 x 10-24g. Also, an atom of carbon has a mass of 1.99 x 10-23g. Instead of using the actual masses of atoms, relative atomic masses (Ar) are use. At first the hydrogen atom was use as the standard against which the masses of other atoms were compared. This was because the hydrogen atom with a mass of one was the lightest known atom. The relative atomic mass was therefore defined as the "number of times one atom of an element is heavier than one atom of hydrogen".

Later on oxygen was used to replace hydrogen as a standard.

The modern method of finding relative atomic masses is by the use of a mass spectrometer. The most accurate

measurements are made with volatile compounds of carbon. The carbon-12 isotope is therefore, used as a standard for defining relative atomic mass. The carbon-12 isotope was chosen as the standard to replace hydrogen and oxygen because:

  • Carbon is a very common element
  • The carbon-12 isotope is very stable
  • Carbon is a solid and is easily handled and transported

On the carbon-12 scale, atoms of the carbon-12 isotope are assigned a relative atomic mass of 12. The relative atomic masses of all other elements are obtained by comparing with the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

The relative atomic mass of an element is the number of times the average mass of one atom of it is heavier than one-twelfth the mass of one atom of carbon-12 isotope

One cannot correctly refer to a 'molecule of sodium chloride'. For ionic compounds, the term formula unit is used to describe the ions which make up the compound. The formula Mass is 58.5. However the term molecule of sodium chloride is often used because most compounds are neither entirely ionic nor covalent.

c) Molar Mass

The molar mass of any substance is the mass of one mole of the substance. The symbol is M. The term molar mass applies to both element and compound. The molar mass of an element is the relative atomic mass expressed in grams. The unit of molar mass is gram per mole (gmol-1). For example the molar mass of copper is 63.5gmol-1. The molar mass of sodium hydroxide is 40gmol-1

d) Avogadro Constant (L)

The molar mass of all elements contains the same number of atoms. The mass of a single carbon-12 atom from mass spectrometry is 1.993 x 10-23g. The number (L) of carbon atoms in one mole of carbon is given by

This value of 6.02 x 10-23mol-1 is known as the Avogadro constant.

The Avogadro constant is the number of atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon 12 isotope

We can count out 6.02 x 1023 atoms of each element by weighing out the relative atomic mass.

1.2 calculation of relative molecular mass of compounds

The relative molecular mass of a compound is the sum of the relative atomic masses of all the atoms in one molecule of the compound.

Example

Calculate the relative molecular mass of the following compounds

a) Ferrous ammonium sulphate, FeSO4(NH4)2SO4.6H2O

b) Tranquilityle, Fe8Zr2Si3Ti3O24

c) Aluminium sulphate, Al2(SO4)3

answer

a) The compound contains 1 Fe, 2 S, 14 O, 2 N, 2 OH

Mr = 56 + (32 x 2) + (16 x 14) + (14 x 2) + (1 x 20)

= 392

b) The compound contains 8 Fe, 2 Zr, 3 Si, 3 Ti, 24 O

Mr = (56 x 8) + (91 x 2) + (28 x 3) +(48 x 3) + (16 x 24)

= 1242

c) The compound contains 2 Al, 3 S, 12 O

Mr = (27 x 2) + (32 x 3) + (16 x 12)

= 342
